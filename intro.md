# Introduction

Autonomous driving scenes are large and information dense. It contains
many objects and regions that are not equally important to the ego
vehicle. A guidance signal of risk that highlights which parts of the
scene deserve greater attention is therefore critical for safe and
efficient decision-making. While proximity is an important factor,
distance alone or semantic alone is insufficient to characterize risk:
some nearby objects such as road surfaces or static background pose
little hazard, whereas other semantic classes, including pedestrians or
vehicles, can represent significant collision risk even they're still
far away. Effective risk-aware perception must therefore jointly reason
about semantic context and geometric proximity. The goal of this project
is to predict a dense per pixel risk map for urban driving scenes where
higher values correspond to regions that pose greater risk level to the
ego vehicle. We construct ground-truth risk labels by integrating
semantic obstacle information with geometric proximity by assigning
higher risk to closer, safety-critical objects while suppressing
low-risk semantic regions. We will use Cityscapes public
autonomous-driving dataset and train a convolutional neural network to
estimate this risk map from visual inputs. This continuous risk
representation is more informative than binary obstacle detection and
provides a useful signal for safety-aware reasoning in autonomous
driving systems.

# Background & Related Work

The Cityscapes and KITTI datasets provide widely used benchmarks for
autonomous driving, offering high-quality semantic annotations and
geometric information that support pixel-level information in urban
environments [@cordts2016cityscapesdatasetsemanticurban]
[@Geiger2012CVPR]. Our task of predicting risk maps is closely related
to semantic segmentation by extending segmentation from learning
semantics to level of risk. This prediction is also object oriented
which share the same pipeline as segmentation models do. Early semantic
segmentation for autonomous driving was dominated by encoder--decoder
architectures such as U-Net shape. DeepLabv3+ builds on this paradigm by
combining hierarchical feature extraction with spatially precise
decoding, achieving strong performance on benchmarks like Cityscapes
[@chen2018encoderdecoderatrousseparableconvolution]. More recently,
transformer-based models have gained prominence: SegFormer replaces
convolutional encoders with hierarchical transformers for global context
modeling, while Mask2Former introduces a unified transformer framework
for dense segmentation and reaches state-of-the-art performance on
Cityscapes [@xie2021segformersimpleefficientdesign]
[@cheng2022maskedattentionmasktransformeruniversal]. More recently,
Vision Transformer (ViT)--based segmentation models have gained
attention for their ability to capture long-range global context, which
is particularly beneficial for dense prediction tasks in complex urban
scenes, as demonstrated by models such as the Segment Anything Model
[@kirillov2023segment].

# Data Processing

We use a combination of the Cityscapes and KITTI datasets as sources of
urban driving scenes [@cordts2016cityscapesdatasetsemanticurban]
[@Geiger2012CVPR]. Cityscapes primarily captures dense urban
environments, while KITTI contains more suburban and rural scenes,
allowing the model to learn risk-aware representations across diverse
driving contexts. Since neither dataset provides dense ground-truth
depth for every pixel, We generate dense depth maps using foundation
depth model. To obtain ground-truth risk labels, we first assign each
semantic class $c$ a fixed semantic danger weight $w_c$, reflecting its
potential hazard to the ego vehicle. For example, pedestrians and
vehicles are assigned higher weights than static roadside objects. We
then compute a depth-based weight to concentrate on closer hazards:
$$g(D)=\text{clip}\left(\frac{1/D - 1/D_{\max}}{1/D_{\min}-1/D_{\max}},\,0,\,1\right),$$
The final dense risk map served as supervision is computed per pixel as:
$$R(x,y)= w_{c(x,y)} \cdot g(D(x,y)),$$

# Architecture

We adopt a U-Net style encoder-decoder CNN. The encoder consists of
stacked convolutional layers that progressively downsample the input to
extract hierarchical semantic features. The decoder upsamples these
features back to the original image resolution. Skip connections between
corresponding encoder and decoder layers preserve fine spatial details,
which are critical for accurately localizing small but safety-critical
objects such as pedestrians. The network takes RGB-D inputs and outputs
a single-channel risk map that takes loss with our artificial GT risk
map. Transformer based segmentation models are effective at modeling
global context while their higher computational and memory cost
motivates the use of a lightweight CNN architecture for this project.

# Baseline Model

As a learning-based baseline, we employ a simple CNN that directly
predicts the dense risk map without using an encoder-decoder structure.
The baseline consists of a small stack of convolutional layers with ReLU
activations that preserve the input spatial resolution followed by a
sigmoid activation to output a single-channel risk map. Unlike the
proposed U-Net architecture, this baseline does not perform multi-scale
feature extraction and has limited capacity to recover fine spatial
details.

# Ethical Considerations

This project uses public captured autonomous driving datasets that apply
automated Gaussian blur procedures to mitigate privacy concerns such as
blurring faces and license plates. However, imperfect detection or
misidentification may still affect individuals' privacy or self-image
particularly if anonymization fails in rare cases. In addition, the
generated risk maps are derived from heuristic assumptions about
semantic danger and proximity which may not fully capture real-world
driving complexity. Mislabeling or overconfidence in the predictions
could contribute to unsafe decisions and serious traffic accidents.
